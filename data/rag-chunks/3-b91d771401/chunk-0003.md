Propulsion System
The propulsion system acts as the reaction control system of the satellite in geostationary
orbit. It generates forces required to move the satellite to its assigned orbital position,
maintain it there, and preserve the correct spin-axis orientation against perturbing forces.
A typical satellite propulsion system consists of three units:
1. Low-thrust actuators: Used for attitude and orbit corrections, providing an
annual velocity increment of approximately 50 m/s.
2. High-thrust motor: Provides the velocity increment necessary for geostationary
orbit injection at the apogee of the transfer orbit.
3. Launch vehicle or shuttle propulsion: Used to inject the satellite into the
transfer orbit.
Among these, low-thrust actuators are most critical for geostationary satellites, as
they maintain correct orbit and attitude throughout the satellite’s operational lifetime.
Low-thrust actuators may be:
• Chemical thrusters: Provide thrust levels ranging from 0.5 N to several thousand
newtons. High-temperature gases produced by chemical reactions of solid or liquid
propellants are expelled through a nozzle.
• Electric thrusters: Produce thrust in the range of 2–10 mN by accelerating
ionized mass in electromagnetic or electrostatic fields.
In communication satellites, chemical thrusters are most commonly used.
Attitude Control System
The attitude control system ensures correct antenna pointing toward the Earth. Gravi-
tational forces from the Sun, Moon, and planets generate rotational moments when the
satellite is not perfectly balanced.
Attitude control is achieved using control loops based on error detection and cor-
rection, with information exchanged through the Telemetry, Tracking, and Command
(TT&C) subsystem. Attitude control may be classified as:
• Passive attitude control
• Active attitude control
