All spacecraft are also equipped with energy storage batteries to supply power during
launch and during eclipse periods when sunlight is blocked. For geostationary orbit (GSO)
satellites, eclipses occur twice a year around the spring and autumn equinoxes, when the
Earth’s shadow passes across the spacecraft. Daily eclipses begin approximately 23 days
before the equinox and end approximately 23 days after.
The duration of daily eclipses increases by a few minutes each day to reach a maximum
of about 70 minutes on the equinox day, after which it decreases by a similar amount
each day.
Sealed nickel–cadmium (Ni–Cd) batteries are commonly used in satellite power sys-
tems due to their high reliability, long operational life, and absence of outgassing during
the charging cycle. Nickel–hydrogen (NiH2) batteries, which offer a significantly improved
power-to-weight ratio, are also widely employed.
The power subsystem additionally includes a power conditioning unit, which is re-
sponsible for battery charge control, power regulation, and system monitoring.
Overall, the power generation and control subsystems constitute a substantial fraction
of the satellite mass, typically accounting for approximately 10–20% of the total dry
weight of a communications satellite.
4. Communications Subsystems
Role of the Communications Subsystem
A communications satellite exists primarily to relay voice, video, and data signals
between earth stations. All other spacecraft subsystems (power, control, telemetry, etc.)
exist solely to support the operation of the communications payload.
Although the communications subsystem may represent only a small fraction of the
total spacecraft mass and volume, it is the only subsystem that generates revenue
for the satellite operator. Consequently, modern satellites are designed to maximize traffic
capacity and spectral efficiency.
Growth of Satellite Capacity
Successive generations of satellites have become larger and more expensive; however, their
traffic capacity has increased at a much faster rate. As a result, the cost per telephone
circuit has steadily decreased with each new generation.
