Polarization Frequency Reuse
• Same frequency band used with orthogonal polarizations
• Effectively doubles available bandwidth
Using these methods, satellites such as INTELSAT V achieved effective bandwidths
far exceeding the originally allocated spectrum.
Allocated Frequency Bands
Satellite designers cannot freely select operating frequencies. Frequency allocations are
governed by international regulations and administered by national authorities (e.g., the
FCC in the United States).
The most widely used bands are:
• C-band: 6/4 GHz
• Ku-band: 14/11 GHz
Although the 6/4 GHz band was expanded from 500 MHz to 1000 MHz, congestion
and interference concerns caused many systems designed before 1985 to use only 500
MHz.
The 14/11 GHz band offers similar bandwidth but is more susceptible to rain atten-
uation, making it less popular in some regions.
Need for Separate Uplink and Downlink Frequencies
Satellite systems always use different frequencies for transmission and reception.
If the satellite transmitted and received at the same frequency:
• The transmitter output would overload the receiver
• Self-interference would occur
Therefore:
• Uplink uses higher frequencies
• Downlink uses lower frequencies

Geostationary Orbit Congestion
The geostationary orbit is a limited resource.
In heavily used regions such as North
America, orbital slots became fully occupied at 3° spacing.
New regulations reduced spacing to 2°, allowing:
• More satellites per arc
• Increased total system capacity
This tighter spacing requires earth stations to use antennas with narrower beams to
avoid interference with adjacent satellites.
Transponder Architecture
A transponder typically consists of:
• Input bandpass filter
• Frequency converter (uplink to downlink)
• High-power output amplifier
Each transponder handles one channel, and multiple transponders operate in parallel
within the satellite.
Modern satellites include:
• Spare transponders
• Switch matrices to reconfigure traffic patterns
This allows flexible allocation of capacity as demand changes.
