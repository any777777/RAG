In passive attitude control, the desired orientation corresponds to a stable equilibrium
position. In active attitude control, the satellite may be unstable or only marginally stable
and requires continuous correction.
The main operations involved in attitude control are:
1. Detection of satellite attitude
2. Comparison with a reference axis
3. Determination of corrective torque
4. Application of correction using onboard actuators
Satellite motion is described using three axes:
• Yaw
• Roll
• Pitch
Satellite Stabilization
To maintain proper attitude in space, satellites are stabilized using momentum wheels
and thrusters along the three principal axes. The two most commonly used stabilization
methods are:
• Spin stabilization
• Three-axis body stabilization
Spin Stabilization
Spin stabilization is widely used in communication satellites. The entire spacecraft is ro-
tated at approximately 30–100 revolutions per minute. This rotation provides gyroscopic
stability that maintains the spin axis in the correct direction.
Such satellites typically have a cylindrical structure covered with solar cells and
thrusters. The transponder is mounted on a despun platform driven by an electric motor
rotating in the opposite direction of the satellite body, ensuring that the antennas con-
tinuously point toward the Earth. The despun section is stabilized using small gas jets
mounted on the periphery of the drum.
