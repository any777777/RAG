Wire Antennas
Wire antennas are primarily used at VHF and UHF frequencies for:
• Telemetry
• Tracking
• Command (TT&C)
Because spacecraft dimensions are often only a few wavelengths at these frequencies,
it is difficult to achieve truly omnidirectional patterns. As a result:
• Nulls may appear in the radiation pattern
• TT&C performance may degrade for certain spacecraft orientations
Careful placement of wire antennas on the spacecraft body is therefore required.
Antenna Patterns and Gain
An antenna radiation pattern describes the variation of field strength with direction in
the far field of the antenna.
Patterns are usually expressed:
• In decibels (dB)
• Relative to the maximum field strength
Antenna Gain
Antenna gain is a measure of the antenna’s ability to concentrate energy in a particular
direction.
High-gain antennas:
• Increase received signal power
• Reduce interference outside the coverage area

Beamwidth
The 3-dB beamwidth is defined as the angular separation between directions in which
the radiated power drops to one half of its maximum value.
Although beamwidth is often quoted, satellite antennas are more accurately described
using contour plots of gain or EIRP over the Earth’s surface.
Effective Isotropic Radiated Power (EIRP)
EIRP combines antenna gain and transmitter output power:
EIRP = Pt × G
When expressed in decibels:
EIRP (dBW) = Pt (dBW) + G (dBi)
To compute received power at an earth station, the EIRP value corresponding to the
station’s location within the antenna pattern must be used.
Horn Antennas
Horn antennas are commonly used:
• At microwave frequencies
• For global or hemispherical coverage
A horn antenna is a flared waveguide that:
• Provides a good impedance match to free space
• Produces relatively wide beams
Typical characteristics:
• Gain up to about 23 dB
• Beamwidths greater than approximately 10°
Horns are also widely used as feeds for reflector antennas.
