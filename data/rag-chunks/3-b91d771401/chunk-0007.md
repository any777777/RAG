• Several hundred sensors are installed on the satellite to monitor parameters such as
fuel tank pressure, voltage and current levels in the power conditioning unit, current
drawn by each subsystem, and critical voltages and currents in the communication
electronics.
• The temperatures of various subsystems must be maintained within predetermined
limits; therefore, a large number of temperature sensors are employed.
• Sensor data and the operational status of each subsystem are continuously reported
to the earth station through the telemetry system.
• Telemetry data are digitized and transmitted using phase shift keying (PSK) on a
low-power telemetry carrier, typically employing time-division multiplexing (TDM)
techniques.
• At the controlling earth station, computers are used to monitor, store, and de-
code telemetry data, enabling immediate assessment of the status of any sensor or
subsystem on the satellite.
• Alarm systems can be activated if any vital parameter exceeds its allowable limits.
Tracking
Tracking techniques are used to determine the current orbit and position of the satellite.
Several methods are commonly employed:
• Velocity and acceleration sensors onboard the satellite can be used to determine
changes in the orbit relative to the last known position by integrating the measured
data.
• The controlling earth station can observe the Doppler shift of the telemetry carrier
signal to determine the rate at which the range between the satellite and the earth
station is changing.
• Active range determination can be achieved by transmitting a pulse or a sequence
of pulses to the satellite and measuring the time delay before the signal is received
back at the earth station.
