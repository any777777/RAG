The Downlink as the Limiting Factor
In most satellite communication systems, the downlink (satellite to earth) is the most
critical part of the link.
This is because:
• Satellite transmit power is severely limited
• The propagation distance is very large (approximately 36,000 km)
• The received power at the earth station is extremely small
Typical received signal powers are on the order of
Pr ≈10−10 W
For reliable communication, the received carrier power must exceed the receiver noise
power by approximately 5 to 25 dB, depending on:
• Signal bandwidth
• Modulation technique
Bandwidth and Power Trade-Off
When satellite transmitter power is low, the only way to maintain the required carrier-to-
noise ratio is to use narrow receiver bandwidths. This limits the information capacity
of the satellite.
Higher transmitter power and directional antennas allow:
• Wider bandwidths

Evolution of Transponder Bandwidth
Early communications satellites employed:
• 250 MHz or 500 MHz wide transponders
• Low-gain antennas
• Transmitter power of 1–2 W
Because earth station receivers could not achieve adequate signal-to-noise ratios over
the full bandwidth, these systems were power-limited.
Modern satellites use:
• Multiple narrower transponders
• Typical bandwidths of 36, 40, or 72 MHz
• Multiple active transponders (12–24 per satellite)
This approach improves flexibility and reduces distortion effects.
Frequency Reuse for Capacity Expansion
The total capacity of a satellite operating in a fixed frequency band can be increased only
by:
• Increasing bandwidth, or
• Reusing existing bandwidth
Two main frequency reuse techniques are employed:
Spatial Frequency Reuse
• Same frequencies used in different geographic regions
• Achieved using multiple directional beams
