To correct a change in the orbital inclination angle (i), a velocity change perpendicular
to the orbital plane is required. This type of correction consumes more fuel than any
other orbital adjustment. For example, a typical satellite with a mass of approximately
1000 kg may require about 30 kg of fuel to maintain the inclination within ±0.1◦. This
results in a reduction of the available mass for communication payload and consequently
reduces the satellite’s communication capacity.
As a result, most satellite systems do not employ continuous inclination control. In-
stead, satellites are often launched with an initial inclination of approximately 3◦. Due to
the perturbing forces acting on the satellite, the inclination decreases naturally at a rate
of about 0.85◦per year. After three to four years, the orbit approaches the equatorial
plane.
With modern earth stations equipped with steerable antennas, small inclination drifts
can be accommodated through tracking. The fuel savings achieved by avoiding inclination
control can instead be used to support additional transponders, thereby increasing the
overall capacity of the satellite.
