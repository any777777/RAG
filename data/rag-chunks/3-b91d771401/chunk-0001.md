Satellite Subsystems
A communication satellite is an integrated space system composed of several specialized
subsystems. Each subsystem performs a critical function that ensures reliable communi-
cation, orbital stability, and long-term operation in the space environment.
1. Attitude and Orbit Control System (AOCS)
The Attitude and Orbit Control System (AOCS) is used to control the orbit of the satellite
and to maintain its stabilization and position in space. Control actions can be performed
autonomously by the satellite itself or through commands sent from the ground station.
The AOCS consists of four major components:
1. Sensors
2. Propulsion system
3. Attitude control
4. Orbit control
Forces Acting on a Satellite
Several forces act on a satellite and tend to disturb its attitude and orbit beyond permis-
sible limits. These forces include:
1. Asymmetry of the Earth’s gravitational field: The Earth is not a perfect
sphere. It bulges at the equator by approximately 65 km near the longitudes of
165◦E and 15◦W. This asymmetry causes acceleration, requiring corrective thrust
from onboard rocket motors (thrusters) to maintain accurate satellite positioning.
Although the Earth is flattened at the poles by about 20 km, this effect has little
impact on geostationary satellites.
2. Gravitational attraction of the Sun, Moon, and planets: These celestial
bodies generate rotational moments if the satellite is not perfectly balanced. The
Moon, being the nearest massive body, exerts the greatest influence.
3. Pressure due to solar radiation: Solar radiation pressure can alter the orienta-
tion (spin axis) of the satellite.
4. Earth’s magnetic field: If the satellite possesses a net magnetic moment, the
Earth’s magnetic field can exert forces that affect its velocity and orientation.
