1. What is Satellite Communication?
Satellite communication uses artificial satellites as radio relay stations in space to
enable long-distance communication.
• Provides communication links independent of geographical barriers.
• Connects remote, rural, maritime, and global users.
• Supports broadcasting, data, and mobile services.
Main Functions of a Communication Satellite
1. Reception of signals from an Earth station (Uplink)
2. Amplification and frequency translation (via Transponder)
3. Re-transmission toward another Earth station (Downlink)
2. Components of a Satellite System
(a) Space Segment
• Satellite payload
• Transponders
• Antennas
• Power subsystem
• Telemetry system
Transponder = Receiver + Frequency Converter + Power Amplifier

3. Satellite as a Communication Repeater
A satellite works like a microwave repeater in space.
• Typical GEO altitude: 35, 786 km
• Uplink frequency ̸= Downlink frequency
Why different frequencies?
• To avoid self-interference
• To allow simultaneous transmission and reception
4. Advantages and Disadvantages
Advantages
• Wide global coverage
• Quick installation
• Flexible deployment
• Independent of terrain
• Suitable for islands and wide-area broadcasting

Disadvantages
• High satellite and launch cost
• Limited lifetime (10–15 years)
• Propagation losses and rain attenuation
• Long propagation delay (especially GEO)
