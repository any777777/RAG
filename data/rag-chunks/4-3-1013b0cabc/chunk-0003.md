4.6 Uplink Design
The design of the uplink is, in many circumstances, more straightforward than the design
of the downlink. This is because the satellite transponder must be presented with an
accurately controlled carrier power level, and it is often practical to use much higher
power transmitters at earth stations compared to what is possible onboard the satellite.
As discussed in the previous section on DBS-TV systems, the uplink transmitter can
typically operate with tens or even hundreds of watts, whereas satellite transmitters are
power-limited by solar arrays and thermal constraints.
However, in Very Small Aperture Terminal (VSAT) networks and mobile satellite
systems, the uplink becomes challenging. VSAT terminals often use antennas with di-
ameters below 1 m and transmitter powers below 5 W, resulting in a very low uplink
effective isotropic radiated power (EIRP). Satellite telephone handsets may be restricted
to transmit powers below 0.5 W to limit radiation exposure to the user, and in many
mobile satellite systems, the user-to-satellite uplink is the weakest link in terms of carrier-
to-noise ratio (CNR).
