## 1.1 Problem Statement & Purpose

## 1.1 Problem Statement and Objective

### Problem

This project targets a **curriculum-bound** learning environment where the quality of an answer is not measured by linguistic fluency, but rather by its **conformity to authoritative references** and its **verifiability** at the level of a specific page, unit, or official source. In such contexts, using a general language model (LLM) only leads to a critical operational gap: while the model may produce linguistically coherent and convincing answers, these answers are often **untraceable to explicit sources** and may deviate from the definitions, procedures, or constraints of the curriculum, or even venture into other contexts that could distract the student. This manifests as a combination of the risk of linguistic distortion and reduced verifiability, both of which conflict with formal educational assessment. Previous research has identified this limitation as a key driver for adopting Retrieval-Enhanced Generation (RAG) technology in educational applications [1], [6], [13], [17].

In practice, the learning materials targeted by this project are mostly distributed as PDF files containing a mix of digitally generated text, scanned pages, and embedded images. This variation significantly limits the effectiveness of traditional text-based search and retrieval methods. Without reliable text extraction, large portions of the curriculum content remain virtually unavailable to subsequent retrieval components. Consequently, optical character recognition (OCR) becomes a prerequisite, not merely an optional addition; without it, semantic retrieval relies on an incomplete and biased representation of the knowledge base, undermining the reliability of any generated answer [2, 6].

## Impact

When learning answers are generated without explicit and verifiable references, several negative effects arise. First, the cost of manual verification increases, as students or instructors must independently search multiple documents to validate claims. Second, user trust diminishes, since the system provides no tangible evidence that can be examined or challenged. Third, and most importantly, there is an increased risk of reinforcing misconceptions or misleading information, particularly in assessment-sensitive contexts such as crucial examinations. These consequences have been consistently highlighted in studies addressing the risks of generative AI in formal education [13, 16, 17].

This is not merely a user experience concern; it also presents a knowledge management challenge. The lack of implementable incorporation and tracking mechanisms hinders systematic control over the knowledge presented, its justification, and its alignment with officially approved educational materials.

### Objective

The first graduation project (GP1) aims to design and implement an academically usable prototype of an educational assistant that provides evidence-based answers using the Retrieval and Regeneration (RAG) methodology. The system specifically aims to:

1. Assimilate curriculum materials provided in both original and scanned PDF formats.

2. Retrieve the most important k content segments related to the curriculum, supported by structured metadata suitable for citation.

3. Generate answers with clear citation links (original document, page, and content segment), enabling efficient verification and auditing [1], [2], [13].

### Scope of Work

**Within Scope of Work (GP1):**

- Build a knowledge base of curriculum-aligned PDF documents, including optical character recognition (OCR) processing where required [2].

- Implement a preprocessing workflow that includes text consolidation, content segmentation, and metadata mapping, followed by embedding generation and indexing within a vector repository to support semantic retrieval [1], [6].

- Generate **evidence-supported answers** with traceable citations that directly link claims to source materials [1], [13].

- Develop a preliminary assessment framework to evaluate retrieval quality, answer validity, and citation accuracy on a representative set of methodological questions [3], [6].

**Outside the scope of the project (GP1):**

- Ensure accurate and error-free answers are obtained from the standalone learning management system, or ensure the absolute validity of answers to all possible queries [6], [17].

- Support questions that fall outside the scope of the indexed methodological materials.

- Advanced agent behaviors or tool-oriented extensions, which are deferred to later phases of the project.

### Success Criteria

Project success is assessed using **measurable and verifiable indicators**, including:

- **Recall performance:** Recall rate (Recall@k) and rate of access to correct answers, measured against a set of categorized questions and evidence [6], [13].

- **Quality of Answers:** Classifying answers as correct, partially correct, or incorrect, using predefined evaluation criteria [3].

- **Citation Accuracy:** The percentage of answers that accurately support their citations with relevant correspondence.

- **Operational performance:** Average and P95 response latency, along with observed failure rates related to OCR processing or empty retrieval results [2], [6].

## 1.2 Background

### 1.2.1 From Large Language Models Only to Retrieval-Enhanced Generation (RAG)

Large Language Models (LLMs) demonstrate superior capabilities in natural language generation and contextual inference. However, they do not necessarily guarantee strict adherence to a specific reference frame, nor do they automatically incorporate domain knowledge updates added after model training is complete. Consequently, LLMs may produce fluent but inconsistent responses that do not conform to curriculum definitions, procedures, or constraints, particularly in formal educational settings where accuracy and traceability are paramount [1] ,[13].

Retrieval-enhanced generation (RAG) emerged around 2020 as a system-level response to these limitations. Instead of relying solely on the model's internal criteria, RAG retrieves relevant evidence from an external knowledge base and constrains the generation process using this retrieved context. By linking responses to explicit sources, RAG improves verifiability, reduces the risk of hallucinations, and mitigates the effects of knowledge obsolescence over time. This paradigm shift—from implicit memory-based generation to evidence-retrieved generation—has gained widespread recognition as a practical and scalable approach for educational and knowledge-intensive applications [1] ,[6] ,[13].

### 1.2.2 PDF-Based Data and Optical Character Recognition (OCR) as a Practical Obstacle

In real-world educational applications, source materials are rarely presented as clean, machine-readable text. Instead, curriculum content is often distributed as heterogeneous PDF documents combining original digital text, scanned pages, graphs, and tables. This reality presents a structural problem: without reliable text extraction, semantic retrieval mechanisms result in an incomplete representation of underlying knowledge, leading to poor retrieval and unreliable subsequent generation.

Experimental studies and experience reports on building RAG systems from PDFs consistently indicate that the quality of Optical Character Recognition (OCR), content segmentation strategies, and metadata design are critical factors in the overall system performance. Errors occurring during OCR propagate through the retrieval path, while inappropriate segmentation boundaries or insufficient metadata reduce the system's ability to identify and cite relevant evidence. Consequently, OCR and preprocessing should be treated as core system components, not secondary steps, with their outputs explicitly measured and monitored to avoid silent retrieval and response generation failures. This aspect of the project is central to data processing [2] ,[6].

### 1.2.3 From RAG Pathways to Proxy Workflows and MCPs

Recent developments in language and model-based systems move beyond fixed question-and-answer paths toward proxy workflows, where models efficiently plan, invoke tools, evaluate intermediate results, and interact with multiple external resources. In educational contexts, these systems can support tasks such as automated assessment, content updating, and multi-source inference, increasing system flexibility and functionality [11] ,[12].

In this context, the Model Context Protocol (MCP) was proposed as a unified mechanism for linking language models to external tools and data sources. By replacing ad hoc integrations with a structured client-server protocol, the MCP addresses the interoperability complexity (N×M problem) of integrating multiple models with multiple tools, while improving interoperability and modularity [7],[14]. However, providing access to tools also expands the range of system vulnerabilities. Previous analyses have identified risks such as injecting malicious code, tools with excessive privileges, and unintended tool execution, all of which can compromise system integrity if not explicitly controlled [7], [8], [9].


Therefore, any transition from RAG-only pipelines to tool-enabled or proxy-based systems necessitates a security-conscious design approach from the outset. This includes principles such as the principle of least privilege, an explicitly allowed tool list, and auditable tool invocation. These considerations make MCP not a default improvement, but a conditional extension whose benefits must be weighed against its operational and security implications.

Why is this important?

Because this specialized protocol for data transfer between LLM and other tools is what makes the project—a communications engineering project—a data processing project in the OCR (Optical Character Recognition) component.

## 1.4 How Has the Problem Been Solved Till Now?

Historically, access to educational information has been addressed through a range of increasingly automated approaches, each reflecting the limitations and technological priorities of its time. Early solutions relied on manual searches and fixed summaries, which offered complete source transparency and ease of implementation, but were not highly scalable and imposed significant verification costs on learners and educators.

With the digitization of educational content, keyword-based text search methods (such as BM25) have become widespread. These methods perform well when source documents are clear and well-organized; however, they struggle with conceptual or procedural questions, and their efficiency drops considerably when OCR output is distorted, as is common in scanned PDFs [6].

More recently, general assistance systems based on learning management software have improved user interaction and reduced the difficulty of querying educational content. Despite these advantages, its unconstrained generative nature exposes it to the risks of misleading and weak verifiability, making it unsuitable for curricula that require explicit conformity to authoritative sources [1], [17].

**Retrieval-enhanced generation (RAG)** represents a shift toward evidence-based responses by combining semantic retrieval and constrained generation. This approach offers a better balance between ease of use and reliability by linking responses to retrieved source material. However, experimental studies consistently show that RAG performance is highly sensitive to pre-existing factors such as input quality, optical character recognition (OCR) accuracy, segmentation strategy, and retrieval configuration, making its success dependent on careful pipeline design and scaling [1], [2], [6], [13].

Alternative strategies, such as **tuning** language models to domain-specific data, can improve stylistic consistency and response coherence within a defined domain. However, fine-tuning does not automatically solve the problem of source documentation, nor does it efficiently handle frequent content updates, limiting its suitability for dynamic educational curricula [5], [6].

Finally, **agent workflows** extend RAG systems by enabling models to plan actions, invoke tools, and coordinate multiple resources for tasks such as assessment, content updates, or multi-step inference. While this model expands system capabilities, it also increases operational and security risks, necessitating clear governance mechanisms, standardized protocols, and strict access controls to ensure system continuity in educational applications [7], [11] ,[12].

|Approach family|Primary strength|Operational limitation|

## 1.5 Main Solution Idea

The core idea of ​​the proposed solution is to build a Data-Enhanced Retrieval (RAG) system based on PDF files, where the learning response is produced as a result of a controlled pathway—retrieval → evidence → constrained generation—rather than unconstrained text generation. In this design, the role of the language model is explicitly limited to synthesizing information previously retrieved from approved curriculum sources, prioritizing traceability and verifiability over linguistic freedom [1] ,[13].

In practice, the system follows a phased approach. First, during assimilation, the curriculum materials provided as PDFs are processed to extract text, with Optical Character Recognition (OCR) technology selectively applied to the scanned pages. Each extracted fragment is explicitly linked to the original document and page to preserve the text source from the initial stage [2]. Second, in the **indexing** phase, the processed content is reconstituted, divided into semantically coherent segments, converted into vector representations, and stored in a vector database with structured metadata. This metadata supports both semantic filtering and subsequent citation generation [1] , [6].

When querying, the **retrieval** component performs a semantic search of the best k results to identify the most relevant pieces of evidence to the given question. Where higher accuracy is required, an optional reordering step can be applied, considering the trade-off between improving retrieval accuracy and increasing computational response time [6]. The **generation** phase then produces an answer clearly constrained by the retrieved evidence, accompanied by clear and traceable citations linking each claim to its supporting source [1] , [13].

Finally, the solution is designed with **governance and scalability** in mind. If tool-based capabilities or external integrations are introduced via the Model Context Protocol (MCP), their use is subject to clear policies such as low-privilege access control and auditable tool invocation. This ensures that any increase in system capabilities does not come at the expense of operational integrity or security [7], [14].

## 1.6 Key Technical Details & Evaluation Summary

### Key Technical Details (High Level)

The proposed system features a range of technical design options that directly support evidence-based responses and measurable performance. First, PDF comprehension is considered a core capability, not a secondary one. The system supports both native text and scanned PDFs, with OCR (Optical Character Recognition) clearly documented and monitored as a critical quality element due to its direct impact on subsequent retrieval performance [2].

Second, the text segmentation strategy is designed to balance contextual completeness with noise reduction. Very large segments tend to weaken semantic relevance during retrieval, while very small segments risk losing the educational context. Consequently, segment size and overlap are treated as adjustable design criteria, and their impact is empirically evaluated, consistent with findings from systematic RAG assessments [6].

Third, each content module is indexed with a **simplified but structured metadata scheme** to enable citation tracking and filtering of results during retrieval. The metadata fields include the document ID (`doc_id`), page number (`page_no`), curriculum classification (`subject` or `unit`), language tag (Arabic or English), and the `content_type` tag, which indicates whether the text is from the original PDF content or generated by Optical Character Recognition (OCR) (`text` / `ocr`). This scheme follows the practical design recommendations of RAG systems focused on PDFs and supports both accountability and control during retrieval. This approach is merely a method we may use for documentation and ease of reviewing results, and non-compliance is not detrimental [2].

Fourth, **retrieval is restricted** to minimize confusion caused by subject and language differences, which are common in educational settings. Metadata-based filters (such as subject/unit and language) are applied before a similarity search, after which semantic retrieval is performed for the best k results. Retrieval effectiveness is measured using standard metrics such as k-accuracy rate and k-retrieval rate to assess whether valid supporting evidence appears among the retrieved results [6] , [13].

Finally, the **answer validation** principle is strictly enforced. Answers are generated only when sufficient supporting evidence is retrieved; otherwise, they are explicitly marked as unsupported. This design choice prioritizes accuracy and transparency over completeness and aligns with best practices for reducing distortion in RAG-based systems [1] , [13].

### Assessment Summary 

The assessment is treated as an **engineering verification process**, not a demonstration. The assessment framework is designed to isolate and measure the performance of each major system component.

- **Retrieval Assessment:** Retrieval quality is assessed using k-retrieval and k-retfall rates on a selected set of questions with known supporting texts, enabling an objective analysis of evidence coverage [6] , [13].

- **Response Evaluation:** Generated responses are categorized using a rating system based on specific criteria (correct, partially correct, incorrect), with brief justifications provided to link errors to retrieval or preprocessing decisions [3].

- **Citation Evaluation:** Citation accuracy is verified by ensuring that cited sources accurately support the corresponding claims and that references cite the correct document and page number. This step ensures that the tracking is not merely nominal but functionally correct [1] , [13].

## 1.8 High-Level Architecture (Conceptual)

The proposed system follows a data path-oriented architecture, designed to enhance evidence documentation and traceability throughout the response generation process, consistent with established retrieval-enhanced response (RAG) system designs [1] , [13]. Typically, the learning content is presented as a variety of PDF files, including original text and scanned sources. These files enter an ingestion phase where the text is extracted, and optical character recognition (OCR) is selectively applied where necessary to ensure content clarity—a prerequisite for effective retrieval in PDF-based RAG systems [2] , [6].

The extracted content then undergoes preprocessing, including text consolidation, semantic segmentation, and the assignment of structured metadata. Each content segment is indexed using vector embeddings and stored in a vector database, thus forming the system's searchable knowledge base. This indexing strategy aligns with common RAG paths that combine embeddings and metadata to support semantic retrieval and traceable citation [1], [6]. During the query, the retrieval component performs a semantic search for the best k results, which can be restricted by metadata filters (such as subject or language) to reduce cross-subject confusion. A reordering stage is added when higher accuracy is required, at the expense of increased response time [6] , [13].

The retrieved evidence is passed to the builder layer, which gathers contextual input for the language model, explicitly including both supporting content and citation mapping. The language model then generates a constrained answer based on this retrieved evidence, producing a framed response with traceable citations linking claims to their original sources [1] , [13]. The final output is presented to the user via a user interface or API, maintaining the relationship between the answer and its supporting references.

At all stages, logging, monitoring, and evaluation mechanisms are integrated to collect operational performance indicators, diagnose the causes of failures, and support the systematic evaluation of retrieval quality, answer validity, and citation accuracy [6]. Where appropriate, tool-based plugins can be integrated through standardized interfaces such as the Model Context Protocol (MCP) to improve interoperability, subject to predefined governance and security constraints, including the principle of least privilege and the ability to audit tool invocations [7] , [14].

**Intended Use Scenario:** A student submits a curriculum-related question; the system retrieves relevant evidence from indexed sources; an answer is generated that is clearly constrained by this evidence; and citations are displayed alongside the answer to enable efficient verification and review, as recommended for evidence-based educational RAG applications [13].

# Chapter 2 — Background
## 2.1 Literature Review: RAG , OCR , and MCP 
### 2.1.1 Why LLM-only Is Insufficient for Curriculum-Bound Learning

Recent research consistently indicates that methods relying solely on large language models are unsuitable for curriculum-oriented educational scenarios that demand methodological rigor and verifiability. This deficiency stems from several structural characteristics of large language models.

First, low verifiability remains a key weakness. Answers generated by independent large language models are not necessarily linked to explicit sources, making them difficult to defend or verify in academic settings where claims must be traceable to authoritative sources. In curriculum-oriented education, the lack of source attribution undermines both the reliability of instruction and the integrity of assessment [1], [13].

Second, the negative impact of pressure is a recurring problem, particularly in examination contexts. When presented with incomplete context or when a definitive solution is required, large language models tend to add or fabricate seemingly plausible content to meet the demand. This behavior has been widely documented and poses a direct risk in educational settings where incorrect but confident answers can mislead learners [6] , [17].

Third, training limitations restrict the applicability of machine learning-based learning systems in dynamic curriculum environments. Updates to the curriculum, textbook editions, or examination methods introduced after the model's training period are not automatically reflected in its outputs. Consequently, answers, even if factually correct, may be outdated or inconsistent with the current curriculum unless external reinforcement mechanisms are employed [1] , [13].

In educational systems, these limitations translate directly into operational risks. An unchecked or misleading answer can be more harmful than no answer at all, as it may reinforce misconceptions while appearing authoritative. Thus, studies generally agree that machine learning-based learning systems lack the reliability guarantees necessary for formal, curriculum-aligned learning environments [17].

---
### 2.1.2 RAG as a System Family 

Retrieval-enhanced text generation (RAG) should not be interpreted as a single technique, but rather as a set of systems comprised of multiple interacting layers, each contributing to the reliability and efficiency of the overall processing path. In practical applications, RAG systems are typically built around four fundamental phases.

The first phase is ingestion and preprocessing, which includes acquiring source documents, extracting text, applying optical character recognition (OCR) when scanning documents, and segmenting the content into manageable units through segmentation. Empirical studies confirm that errors or inconsistencies occurring in this phase—particularly due to OCR distortion or incorrect text segmentation—directly affect all subsequent components [2] , [6].

The second phase is indexing, where the processed text segments are converted into vector representations and stored in a vector database. This phase may also include auxiliary indexes or metadata-aware structures to support efficient filtering and retrieval. The choice of embedding models and indexing strategies plays a pivotal role in determining retrieval quality, particularly in multilingual or specialized educational contexts [1], [6].

The third stage consists of **retrieval and reordering**. Upon input of a user query, the system retrieves the best k of the most relevant pieces of evidence using semantic similarity. In environments requiring higher accuracy, a reordering step can be applied to reorder the retrieved results based on a deeper assessment of relevance, improving accuracy at the expense of increasing computational load [6].

The final stage is **mediated generation**, where the language model generates an answer explicitly constrained by the retrieved evidence. Crucially, this stage should include a defined policy for handling insufficient or low-quality evidence, such as withholding an answer or indicating uncertainty, rather than generating unsupported content [1], [13].

A key conclusion drawn from previous studies is that **the performance of a RAG system is not attributable to the language model alone**. Instead, the quality of the system is determined by the cumulative effect of design decisions across the entire processing path—from the accuracy of optical character recognition and the data segmentation strategy, to the retrieval configuration and the integrity of evidence. Weaknesses at any single stage can propagate and undermine the verifiability of the final answer, reinforcing the need to treat the RAG system as an end-to-end integrated system rather than a model-level improvement [2], [6].

---
### 2.1.3 OCR as the Practical Bottleneck

Practical reports on building RAG systems from PDFs consistently indicate that **PDF-centric processing is a major operational hurdle.** Unlike formatted text collections, educational materials are often distributed as scanned documents or image-rich PDFs, making it impossible to extract large portions of content using standard text analysis techniques and necessitating the use of Optical Character Recognition (OCR) [2].

However, OCR presents its own challenges. **OCR distortion,** including character-level errors, formatting distortions, and loss of structural information, can significantly degrade semantic information retrieval performance. Empirical analyses show that even moderate OCR inaccuracy can lead to decreased recall, false similarity matches, and unstable retrieval arrangements, particularly in technical educational content or content with many equations [2], [6].

Furthermore, **tables, figures, and graphs** are common forms of semi-textual or non-textual knowledge found in educational PDFs. These elements require clear processing strategies, as directly converting them to text could lead to the insertion of misleading code, while completely excluding them risks losing essential educational information. Studies emphasize the need for RAG systems focused on PDFs to adopt a clear policy regarding how this content is processed, converted, or deleted during the input process [2].

Consequently, many studies advocate treating Optical Character Recognition (OCR) not as a hidden preprocessing step, but as a measurable system component. By isolating OCR as a separate stage with its own metrics and recordings, system designers can prevent "silent failures" where poor OCR quality goes unnoticed into the retrieval and creation stages. This perspective reframes OCR quality as a fundamental factor in the reliability of RAG systems, rather than a secondary implementation detail [2], [6].

### 2.1.4 MCP and Tool-Enabled Agents (Interoperability and Risk)

As RAG-based systems evolve toward more interactive workflows, a central question arises: how can language models be linked to multiple external tools and resources without resorting to complex, customized integrations for each new capability? The Model Context Protocol (MCP) has been proposed as a unified client-server interface that enables language models to interact with tools and data sources through a standardized abstraction, thereby reducing integration complexity and improving interoperability between heterogeneous systems [14].

The MCP literature highlights several potential benefits of this approach, including lower integration costs, improved tool reusability, and a clearer separation between model logic and external capabilities. By providing a common protocol layer, the MCP aims to mitigate the N×M integration problem that arises when multiple models must interact with multiple tools in customized ways [7], [14]. These characteristics are particularly relevant for educational systems that may gradually integrate assessment tools, content management systems, or external knowledge services.

However, previous analyses also confirm that **tool-enabled agents add new categories of operational and security risks.** One widely discussed threat is **malicious instruction injection,** either direct or indirect, where malicious instructions embedded in user input or retrieved content influence the model's decision to invoke tools or alter its intended behavior [7]. Another risk is **tools with excessive privileges,** where tools granted excessive privileges can inadvertently expose sensitive data or perform actions beyond their intended scope, thus amplifying the impact of model errors or malicious instructions [7], [8]. A third concern is **tool output poisoning,** where retrieved tool outputs are reintroduced into the model context and exploited to bypass safeguards or manipulate subsequent inference steps [7] ,[9].

Consequently, studies agree that tool integration cannot be considered a purely functional improvement. Any architecture based on a model control protocol or enabled by tools must be based on clear governance principles from the outset. These include **minimal privilege access control**, **a strict list of permitted tools**, and **comprehensive logging of tool calls**. It is important to note that these mechanisms are formulated as **core design requirements**, not as optional optimization steps to be implemented after deployment [7], [14].

## 2.2 Context and Importance (Educational Setting)

In formal educational settings, such as curriculum-based and examination-based systems, the concept of a correct answer extends beyond general knowledge. An acceptable answer is expected to conform to the formal definitions, established procedures, and approved problem-solving methods outlined in the curriculum. In this context, previous studies on the application of Retrieval-Enhanced Generating (RAG) in education emphasize that the core value of AI-assisted learning systems lies not in conversational fluency, but in the alignment and transparency of references—that is, the ability to link answers to authoritative sources [13].

At the same time, the adoption of AI tools in education presents a dual dynamic. On the one hand, these tools offer tangible opportunities, including improved interpretive support, personalized learning pathways, and reduced teaching workload. On the other hand, studies consistently highlight significant challenges, such as over-reliance on AI, threats to academic integrity, and concerns regarding data privacy and responsible use [16],[18]. These challenges highlight the need for educational AI systems to move beyond simply generating answers and to clearly define their operational boundaries, specifying when the system should provide an answer, when it should refrain or request clarification, and how it should present supporting evidence to the learner [13], [17].

In the Jordanian context, the existence of official educational platforms reflects institutions' interest in employing digital tools to support learning processes [15]. However, this project does not assume the capabilities, coverage, or accessibility of existing platforms, nor does it rely on proprietary integration processes. Instead, it focuses on developing an evaluable, curriculum-aligned prototype, whose design options and performance characteristics can be independently assessed. This prototype provides a solid foundation for future consideration of its integration with approved systems or licensed educational content, in accordance with policies, governance, and regulatory constraints [15], [17].

## 3.1 Design Overview

### 3.1.1 Describe your project design to achieve the solution

#### a) Design Objectives

The system was designed to achieve **technically defensible objectives** within the context of language-based learning assistants, while addressing known limitations related to hallucinations, multilingual performance, and the variability in source quality [1]. The main design objectives are summarized as follows:

1. **Evidence-Based (Reference-Supported) Answers:**

Ensuring that each generated answer is based on the retrieved curriculum content and accompanied by clear references (source document and page number). This objective aligns with established practices for reducing hallucinations and improving verifiability in RAG-based systems [1], [6].

2. **Retrieval Efficiency:** Designing the retrieval path so that the correct supporting evidence appears among the top k retrieved candidates with a high probability. This is achieved through semantic retrieval based on embedding, along with measurable effectiveness indicators, enabling an objective assessment of retrieval quality [1], [6].

3. **Rule of Handling Heterogeneous PDFs (Scanned and Original):**

The system takes into account the practical reality that educational materials are often distributed as a mix of original text and scanned PDFs. Accordingly, the system includes Optical Character Recognition (OCR) as a prerequisite for retrieval, rather than as an optional preprocessing step [2], [6].

4. **Arabic-English Suitability:**

The system supports bilingual queries and responses within the local school education context. The design explicitly acknowledges that multilingual performance depends on the choice of embedding model and preprocessing practices; therefore, language handling is treated as a measurable design variable, not a presupposed capability [6], [13].

5. **Interactive Response Time for Educational Use:**

Aim for appropriate response times for short interactive study sessions. Excessive response time is a barrier to adoption and a major contributor to high operating costs, making response time a primary design constraint rather than a secondary optimization goal [6], [16].

Knowledge Updating Capability:** Enable knowledge base updates by re-incorporating and re-indexing documents without requiring model retraining. This design choice reduces maintenance costs and supports curriculum development over time [1], [2].

Tool Management for Model Context Protocol (MCP)-Based Extensions:** When extending the system to use external tools, adopt a unified interface via the Model Context Protocol (MCP), with restricted permissions and audit logs to control operational and security risks associated with tool-enabled agents [7],[10], [14].

8. **Cost-Conscious Operation:**

This system supports phased deployment through an initial pilot strategy, with clear control over key cost factors, specifically LLM inference and OCR processing. This ensures that the system's scalability is evaluated in conjunction with its economic feasibility [6].

#### b) System Limits (Scope and Interfaces)

**Scope Components:**

- Incorporation of curriculum-aligned resources delivered as PDF files, including Optical Character Recognition (OCR) processing for scanned pages when required.

- Text preparation workflow that includes standardization, semantic segmentation, and the attachment of structured metadata (e.g., original document, page number, subject/unit, and language).

- Indexing of content fragments using vector embeddings and storing them in a vector database to support semantic retrieval.

- Retrieval of the best k evidence for each user query, with the ability to filter metadata by subject or unit.

- Generation of **evidence-supported answers** accompanied by clear and traceable references.

- Tools for recording and evaluating processes (evaluation system) to produce auditable metrics related to retrieval quality, answer validity, and citation accuracy.

**Out-of-Border Components (GP1):**

- Training a language model from scratch or performing extensive fine-tuning. This is treated as a design alternative, not an implemented feature.

- Ensuring correct answers for queries that fall outside the scope of indexed curriculum materials.

- Fully production-ready deployment features (such as strict SLAs or high availability guarantees); the system is limited to a design-level specification and a functional prototype or implementation plan.

**External Interfaces and Dependencies:**

- A language model provider, accessible either via an external API or a locally hosted model in later stages.

- An OCR and PDF processing engine used during document ingestion.

- A storage layer for native documents and indexed data during deployment.

- When MCP extensions are enabled, external MCP tool servers display explicitly defined tools with restricted permissions, in accordance with the official MCP specification [14].

c) Advanced Modules

The proposed method consists of a series of functional modules with clearly defined inputs and outputs to facilitate maintenance, expansion, and systematic evaluation. This modular structure embodies the effective practices demonstrated in practical RAG applications of PDF sources [2] and in systematic reviews of RAG methodologies [6].

1. **Document Acquisition and Optical Character Recognition (OCR) Module**

**Function:** Converts PDF sources (including original text and scanned images) into machine-readable text suitable for indexing, while preserving page data accuracy.

Extracts text, page maps, and related information.

2. **Preprocessing and Segmentation Module**

**Function:** Refine and consolidate the extracted content, then segment it into semantically coherent segments supported by metadata to facilitate retrieval and citation.

Content segments are accompanied by metadata records.

3. **Inclusion and Indexing Module**

**Function:** Create vector representations of content segments and store them in a vector database to facilitate semantic similarity searching.

A vector-based knowledge index.

4. **Retrieval Module**

**Objective:** To obtain the most relevant k pieces of evidence for a specific question, using metadata-based filtering when necessary. A reordering stage can be added later if the pilot evaluation shows a significant improvement in accuracy compared to the response time cost [6].

A set of retrieved evidence accompanied by relevance scores and source references.

5. **Question Formulation/Formatting Module**

**Objective:** To formulate the final question by integrating the acquired evidence according to the system's response protocol, which includes documentation requirements and rejection procedures for insufficient evidence [1], [6].

**Output:** A final report and a structured documentation map.

6. **Instructional Response Models Module**

**Objective:** To prepare concise, curriculum-aligned instructional responses for school users, taking into account the provided evidence and ensuring clear documentation.

**Outputs:** Response text accompanied by documentation and confidence indicators (optional).

7. **Model Context Protocol Tools Module (Optional/Phased)**

**Function:** To present system-defined functions as external tools accessible via the Model Context Protocol (MCP), including assessments or data aggregation, with clearly defined and thoroughly reviewed permissions [7],[10],[14].

8. **User Interface/API Component**

**Function:** To provide an interface for end users (students or teachers) to submit inquiries and receive responses with supplementary materials.

9. **Documentation and Evaluation Unit**

**Function:** Documenting system performance, errors, and operational performance indicators, and conducting evaluations to facilitate the development of logical, data-driven foundations for design choices and trade-offs.[6].

### 3.2.2 Design Process

The system design follows a data path-oriented process, clearly separating the ingestion, indexing, and querying phases. This separation minimizes unnecessary reprocessing, supports incremental content updates, and aligns with best practices used in building RAG systems on PDF sources [2].

The design process consists of the following steps:

1. Ingestion
	Curriculum PDF documents are ingested and analyzed to distinguish between original text pages and scanned pages or images.
	
2. Conditional OCR
	Optical Character Recognition (OCR) is selectively applied to scanned pages to convert them into machine-readable text, avoiding unnecessary processing of pages that already contain extractable text.
	
3. Consolidation 
	The extracted text is consolidated to remove encoding inconsistencies and reduce noise (such as duplicate headings, footers, or other unwanted elements) where needed, improving the stability of subsequent retrieval.

4. **Text Segmentation and Metadata Allocation:**
	The unified text is divided into semantically coherent, overlapping segments. Each segment is enriched with structured metadata, enabling accurate retrieval, filtering, and citation tracking.
	
5. **Inclusion and Indexing:**
	Vector inclusions are created for each segment and stored in a vector database, forming the system's searchable knowledge index.
	
6. **Retrieval:**
	Upon querying, the system retrieves the k most relevant segments using semantic similarity, with the option to filter based on metadata. A reordering stage is added only if a pilot evaluation demonstrates a significant improvement in accuracy compared to the additional response time cost [6].
	
7. **Ruling Construction:**
	The retrieved evidence is compiled into a structured directive that enforces the system's answer policy, including mandatory citation and explicit acknowledgment of uncertainty or insufficient evidence where appropriate [1].
	
8. **Generation:**
	The language model generates an evidence-constrained learning response with traceable references.
	
9. **Assessment Tools:**
	An assessment tool is integrated into the workflow to execute predefined sets of questions and record results (e.g., k-point recall, response time, citation accuracy) in structured formats suitable for analysis and comparison [6].
	
10. **Model Context Protocol (MCP) Integration (in stages):**
	Some system capabilities—such as batch data ingestion, assessment execution, or schema generation—can be made available as external tools via the Model Context Protocol (MCP). Access to these tools is subject to explicit permissions and audit mechanisms to control operational and security risks [7–10], [14].
	
From an educational perspective, this path-based process aligns with RAG-based learning applications, which prioritize transparency and evidence-based learning over generating ambiguous answers. Previous studies on RAG learning systems emphasize that making supporting evidence visible and verifiable is essential for responsible use in learning environments, especially when interacting with students and teachers [4], [11]–[13].

### 3.2.4 Design Constraints

The proposed system design is subject to a set of explicit constraints that directly impact architectural design and implementation decisions. These constraints reflect the practical limitations inherent in PDF-centric RAG systems and are treated as fundamental design factors, not merely secondary issues.

**Optical Character Recognition (OCR) Quality Constraint**
In PDF-centric data processing paths, OCR quality is a fundamental constraint. Poor OCR output quality can lead to the retrieval of incorrect or misleading evidence, which in turn affects answer generation. Consequently, OCR errors are not isolated failures but systemic risks that directly impact establishment and verifiability. This dependency has been extensively documented in experimental studies of RAG systems based on scanned documents [2], [6].

**Operational Resource Constraints**
OCR processing, embedding generation, and optional reordering add significant computational costs and response times. Therefore, these components are treated as **measurable trade-off decisions**, not default features. Their activation and configuration must be justified through empirical evaluation to balance retrieval quality, response time, and operational cost.

**Arabic Language Limitations**
Arabic language retrieval performance is particularly affected by the choice of embedding model and preprocessing decisions, including standardization and segmentation. Even slight variations in these steps can lead to nonlinear effects on retrieval accuracy. Consequently, Arabic language support is treated as a constrained design space requiring explicit measurement and validation, rather than assuming parity with English performance [6], [13].

**Search Scope Limitations**
Queries that fall outside the scope of indexed curriculum materials constitute a major limitation. Attempting to answer such questions without supporting evidence significantly increases the risk of confusion. Therefore, the system must detect out-of-scope queries and respond with rejection or redirection, prioritizing accuracy and transparency over completeness of the answer [1].

**Tool security limitations**
When tool-dependent extensions are introduced via the MCP protocol, the system's vulnerability to attacks increases. Excessively broad tool privileges can lead to misuse or code-injection-driven behavior. Consequently, tool integrity is subject to strict access control policies, and any deviation from the principle of least privilege is treated as a design violation, not merely an acceptable risk [7].

Instead, it adopts a set of **practical design standards and engineering best practices** that are directly applicable to the system’s scope and can be verified within the project lifecycle.

**Adherence to MCP specifications (when enabled).**  
When tool-based extensions are introduced, the system follows the official Model Context Protocol (MCP) specifications, including the client–host–server architecture, explicit tool definitions, and structured context exchange. This adherence aims to ensure interoperability and avoid ad hoc integration patterns that complicate maintenance and risk management [14].

**Least-privilege principle for tool access.**  
As a baseline security standard, all tool integrations are governed by the principle of least privilege. Tools are exposed with narrowly scoped permissions, explicit allowlisting, and auditable invocation paths. This practice is consistent with MCP risk analyses and communication-pattern literature, which emphasize permission minimization as a primary control against tool misuse and prompt-injection-driven behaviors [7], [9].

**Reproducibility and re-runnability of the pipeline.**  
The system design emphasizes reproducibility by documenting the ingestion, preprocessing, indexing, and evaluation pipelines such that the knowledge index can be rebuilt and performance re-measured when parameters, data sources, or models change. This standard supports systematic experimentation and longitudinal comparison, and it is operationalized through versioned configurations and repeatable evaluation workflows, to be detailed further in Chapters 4 and 5 [2], [6].

Collectively, these standards provide a **defensible engineering baseline** that aligns with the project’s prototype-oriented scope while enabling future extension toward more formal compliance frameworks as implementation maturity increases.

### 3.2.7 Safety Consideration

In the context of this project, **safety** is defined in terms of **cognitive, operational, and data-related safety**, rather than physical safety. The system is designed to reduce the risk of misinformation, misuse, and unintended operational behavior through explicit policies and architectural controls.

**Cognitive safety.**  
The primary safety objective is to prevent the dissemination of unsupported or misleading information. To this end, the system enforces an **evidence-only answering policy**, whereby any core claim must be grounded in retrieved evidence. When retrieved evidence is insufficient, ambiguous, or out of scope, the system applies a **refusal or redirection policy**, prompting the user to clarify the unit or lesson instead of generating speculative content. This approach directly addresses hallucination risks identified in prior studies on RAG and LLM-based educational systems [1].

**User safety in school environments.**  
Within school settings, safety also includes mitigating risks related to academic misuse and over-reliance on automated assistance. The system interface is intentionally designed to surface sources and page references prominently, encouraging students to consult the underlying material rather than copy a standalone answer. In addition, the system explicitly communicates its limitations, reinforcing its role as a support tool rather than a substitute for learning or assessment. These measures align with recommendations for responsible integration of generative AI in formal education [16], [17].

**Data safety.**  
User interactions, including submitted questions, may constitute sensitive or behavioral data. To reduce exposure, the design prioritizes **data minimization**, limiting data collection and retention to what is strictly necessary for operation and evaluation. The system avoids storing sensitive identifiers and favors anonymization when interaction data are retained for analysis or improvement purposes.

**Tool safety (when MCP is enabled).**  
When tool-based capabilities are introduced via the Model Context Protocol (MCP), additional safety controls are required. The system restricts tool access through explicit allowlisting, prohibits generic or broadly privileged execution tools, and maintains audit logs for all tool invocations. These controls are consistent with MCP threat analyses, which emphasize permission governance and traceability as essential safeguards against tool misuse and prompt-injection-driven behaviors [7].

Collectively, these safety considerations frame the system as a **controlled and transparent educational aid**, prioritizing correctness, accountability, and responsible use over unconstrained automation.

## 4.0 Background and Scope of Implementation

The implementation plan is based on the **Retrieval-Enhanced Generation (RAG)** framework as a practical approach to reducing noise and improving traceability by explicitly linking model outputs to external sources [1]. Since the project data consists primarily of **scanned and varied PDF documents**, the proposed approach incorporates recommendations and lessons learned from pilot studies on building RAG systems from PDFs [2]. A preliminary assessment methodology, based on previous work demonstrating how RAG can improve assessment and scoring tasks, particularly in short-answer learning scenarios, has been incorporated [3].

From an instructional interaction perspective, the system design prioritizes **evidence presentation and transparency** over traditional teaching models. While some previous tools have explored guided or teacher-like interactions, this project avoids adopting educational metaphors that cannot be practically verified. Instead, this approach focuses on verifiable behaviors—such as retrieving curriculum-aligned evidence, restricting the generation process, and displaying references—that have proven effective in early educational applications of RAG systems [4], [13]. Customization is viewed as a future evolutionary path, moving from RAG-based foundations to operational systems only when experimental benefit justifies it, rather than as an inherent feature of the initial application [5].

Design and implementation decisions are further based on systematic literature reviews that standardize RAG techniques, evaluation criteria, and known challenges. These reviews provide a solid foundation for pipeline component selection and measurable success criteria, ensuring that implementation options align with established evidence rather than undocumented practices [6].

Regarding tools, the Model Context Protocol (MCP) is considered a standard integration layer for extensions supported by the tools, with particular attention paid to the security and privacy risks associated with the MCP server lifecycle [7]. Comparative studies of proxy interoperability protocols (including MCP, ACP, A2A, and ANP) are used to avoid ad hoc integrations that hinder scalability and maintainability [8]. Furthermore, design pattern reviews of MCP communications contribute to identifying tools with limited scope and controlled permissions [9]. Because evaluating "agent + tool" behavior is not straightforward, concepts from MCP-AgentBench are used to guide the design of realistic and verifiable assessment workflows in subsequent phases [10].

In the educational field, the scope of implementation aligns with surveys on learning management agents in education and analyses of agent workflows (planning, tool use, and coordination), explicitly acknowledging the operational and administrative challenges identified in these studies [11], [12]. RAG educational surveys, which focus on school-level contexts, also contribute to clarifying the constraints related to curriculum alignment and assessment sensitivity [13]. Official MCP documentation is used to ensure terminological consistency and an accurate architectural interpretation of MCP components [14].

The project also considers compatibility with the Jordanian educational context, including formal learning environments and officially recognized platforms such as Siraj, without assuming direct integration or access to proprietary systems [15]. Broader considerations regarding AI adoption in schools—including opportunities, risks, and institutional strategies—are treated as design constraints rather than ambitious goals [16], [17]. It also acknowledges the practical barriers to personalized learning in AI-assisted education to avoid overestimating the system's capabilities [18]. Finally, the initial deployment references are based on cloud-based operational models for managing confidential data and lightweight hosting (such as Replit/Ripple environments) and are used as purely operational examples, not architectural requirements [19].

### 4.1.1 Technology Stack

#### (A) Hardware and Execution Environment

The system is engineered to function within a containerized, cloud-based execution environment, selected to reduce operational costs, facilitate scaling, and guarantee continuous availability. The system requires network connectivity to function, as it is not designed for offline operation.  
  
**Deployment Model (Container-Based)**
The application is encapsulated and deployed as a containerized service. This methodology ensures environmental uniformity throughout creation, testing, and deployment, while facilitating iterative testing and assessment. Containers facilitate the uniform deployment of system components (including ingestion, retrieval, and evaluation) independent of particular hardware configurations.  
  
**Resource Constraints (CPU, Memory, Optional GPU)**
In the preliminary beta phase, the system prioritizes CPU-based execution as the standard. Resource limitations for CPU and RAM are established at the container level to avert unregulated resource utilization during beta testing. GPU acceleration is an optional enhancement for subsequent phases (GP2), specifically for expediting optical character recognition (OCR) or embedding, and is thus not a requirement.  

**Scaling Policy (Transition from Beta to Release)**    
The initial deployment adheres to a scaling policy that commences with a beta phase, during which the system is set up for restricted concurrent usage while monitoring latency, costs, and failure patterns. Horizontal and resource expansion are postponed until experimental data demonstrate their necessity. This incremental strategy prevents premature optimization while ensuring a definite trajectory towards broader implementation.  

**Management of Confidential Data and Credentials**    
Sensitive configuration elements, like API keys and service credentials, are excluded from the code and container images. Confidential data is managed via environment-level configuration tools offered by the hosting platform. This method conforms to standard cloud deployment guidelines and mitigates the danger of credential exposure during development or deployment.  

**Network and External Credentials**
The system expressly depends on network connectivity to contact external services, such as language model providers and cloud OCR engines when necessary. Components dependent on external APIs are segregated behind clearly defined interfaces to provide future substitution with local or alternative services as required. The system is anticipated to remain operational during a network interruption.

**Development Environments versus Deployment Environments**
In the preliminary testing and development stages, a local machine environment is utilized for swift iteration and debugging. Upon achieving stability, the components are transitioned to containerized services that replicate the intended cloud execution environment. This division enables developmental flexibility while preserving deployment precision.

#### (C) Document Ingestion, OCR, and Preprocessing

**PDF Processing and Optical Character Recognition (OCR):**
The system uses **Docling** software to analyze PDF files and perform OCR processing. This decision stems from the project's extensive reliance on numerous scanned PDF documents, where OCR quality is the primary obstacle affecting the efficiency of subsequent retrieval and the reliability of results [2],[21],[6]. Therefore, OCR is considered a core component of the processing path, not merely a preliminary, secondary stage.

**Processing Methodology:** The receiving process features a clear definition of responsibilities:

- **Extraction Stage (Analysis and OCR):**

	The PDF documents undergo processing to extract the text content from their original pages, selectively using OCR for scanned pages or images. This stage produces consistent text while preserving the original page data.

- **Indexing Stage (Post-Processing):**

	The retrieved text is sent to the pre-processing and post-indexing components without re-executing the OCR technology. By separating the extraction process from indexing, the system reduces unnecessary reprocessing when hashing algorithms, embedding models, or retrieval criteria change, which is crucial during iterative experiments in GP1 [2].

- Metadata Saving:

	The results of the extraction step are saved with structured information, including the source file name, page number, and text type (original or OCR-derived). This metadata is sent through the processing path to facilitate citation tracking in generated responses and to perform diagnostic checks for OCR-related errors [1, 2].

This design identifies document embedding and OCR as measurable and verifiable processes, the quality of which directly impacts system performance. Implementation facilitates systematic evaluation and controlled optimization in later project phases by isolating these phases and maintaining comprehensive metadata.

#### (D) Chunking, Embeddings, and Vector Store

**Segmentation Strategy**
Textual content is divided into overlapping segments by default in the RAG system. This strategy reflects a well-known trade-off: very large segments tend to dilute semantic relevance during retrieval, while very small segments compromise the learning context. Therefore, overlapping is used to maintain content continuity between adjacent segments without excessively increasing the index size [1], [6]. Segment size and overlapping ratios are treated as adjustable parameters and will be determined experimentally in GP2.

**Embedding Models**
The system uses embedding models that support both Arabic and English content. Two alternatives are being considered: (a) a single multilingual embedding model, or (b) separate language-specific embedding models with dual retrieval paths. The final choice is deferred to the second phase of development (GP2) and will be based on controlled experiments, as previous studies indicate that the retrieval performance of Arabic texts can vary considerably depending on data characteristics (e.g., textbooks versus exam questions and answers) and the embedding design [6], [13].

**Vector Database**
**ChromaDB.** is used as a vector database to store embeddings and their associated metadata. In Phase 1 (GP1) and pilot deployments, a permanent local setup is used to simplify experiments, ensure reproducibility, and reduce operational complexity. This setup supports efficient search for the highest k similarity results while maintaining granular control over indexed data during initial evaluation.[22]

**Relational Data Storage**
A lightweight relational data store (such as Firebase) is used to manage non-vector data, including user logs, session metadata, and aggregated usage statistics. This separation of vector and relational data storage promotes clearer data governance and avoids linking user management to the retrieval architecture.

Overall, these choices emphasize **measurability and flexibility.** Hashing parameters, embedding selection, and retrieval settings were intentionally left tunable in GP1 to enable data-driven optimization once representative evaluation sets are available.

#### (E) LLM Layer and Generation Policy

**LLM selection (GP1).**  
During GP1, the system utilizes the **OpenAI API** as the language model backend. This choice prioritizes implementation stability, rapid iteration, and reduced integration risk during early development. Using a managed API allows the project to focus on validating system behavior—such as retrieval grounding, citation fidelity, and latency—without incurring the operational overhead of model hosting and optimization at this stage.

**Target LLM strategy (GP2 / release).**  
For later phases, a **phased migration toward an open-source language model hosted in a cloud environment** is considered. The primary motivation is to reduce long-term operational cost and mitigate dependency on a single commercial provider. This transition is explicitly conditional: an open-source model will be adopted only if it meets a defined minimum quality threshold relative to the API-based baseline, particularly with respect to answer correctness, grounding reliability, and multilingual performance [1], [6].

**Generation policy (evidence-first).**  
The system enforces an **evidence-grounded generation policy**, in which answers are produced only in the presence of sufficient retrieved evidence. Retrieved text segments are embedded into the prompt, and each substantive claim in the generated response is linked to internal references (document and page). The objective is not stylistic richness or conversational fluency, but **reliability and verifiability** appropriate for school-level learners. When supporting evidence is weak or unavailable, the system favors refusal or redirection over speculative generation [1], [13].

This policy positions the language model as a **controlled synthesis component** rather than an autonomous knowledge source, aligning generation behavior with the project’s educational and evaluative requirements.

#### (F) MCP Integration (Tools and Interoperability)

**Role of MCP.**  
The **Model Context Protocol (MCP)** is adopted as a **standardized integration layer** that connects the language model to external tools through explicit interfaces, rather than embedding ad hoc tool logic directly within application code. This separation improves modularity, interoperability, and long-term maintainability, while reducing the complexity associated with bespoke integrations [14].

**Architectural components.**  
The planned MCP-based integration follows a clear client–server separation:

- An **MCP client** embedded within the backend service is responsible for requesting tool execution and handling structured responses.
    
- One or more **MCP servers**, deployed as independent services, expose narrowly scoped tools through well-defined interfaces.
    

This architecture enables independent development, deployment, and auditing of tools without coupling them tightly to the core RAG pipeline.

**Planned tools (GP1/GP2 roadmap).**  
The following tools are identified as candidates for phased exposure via MCP:

1. **`run_evaluation`** — Executes evaluation scripts and returns structured performance reports, supporting reproducible measurement of retrieval quality, answer correctness, and citation fidelity.
    
2. **`generate_diagram`** — Produces explanatory diagrams or visual aids associated with specific curriculum concepts to support instructional clarity.
    
3. **`fetch_media`** — Retrieves approved educational images or videos linked to a defined knowledge segment.
    

The inclusion of each tool is conditional on demonstrated utility and measurable benefit; no tool is assumed to be enabled by default. The mention of these tools here does not guarantee their inclusion in GP2.

**Security posture and governance.**  
Tool integration is governed by a **least-privilege security posture**. Each tool is exposed with explicitly constrained permissions, and tools capable of generic execution or unrestricted access are treated as high-risk and therefore excluded or heavily sandboxed. All tool invocations are subject to input validation and audit logging to ensure traceability and post hoc analysis. Prior analyses of MCP and agent communication protocols emphasize that interoperability gains must not come at the expense of permission control and auditability [7]–[9].

Overall, MCP is treated as an **enabling but optional layer** that supports controlled system evolution. Interoperability is pursued only where it can be reconciled with explicit security constraints and evaluable operational benefits, consistent with the project’s prototype-oriented scope.

#### (H) Logging and Monitoring

**Logging baseline (GP1).**  
In GP1, the system adopts **structured logging** as a mandatory baseline capability. Logs are designed to capture key operational signals required for diagnosis and evaluation, including end-to-end query latency, the number of retrieved evidence chunks, token consumption during generation, and OCR-related errors encountered during ingestion. #### (I) Evaluation Tools

**Assessment Tool Structure.**
The assessment framework is implemented using a set of **Python scripts**, along with structured input/output files. The assessment input consists of a selected set of questions stored in a machine-readable format (such as JSON or CSV), while the assessment output is recorded in CSV files containing measurable metrics, such as response time, retrieval results, answer validity ratings, and citation credibility.

**Reasoning for Methodology.** This design is based on the need for a **clear separation between retrieval quality and generation quality** in the assessment of RAG systems. Previous studies have confirmed that combining these two dimensions leads to misleading conclusions about system performance, as answer invalidities may arise from retrieval errors rather than generation behavior [1], [6]. By recording intermediate and final metrics in dedicated files, the assessment process remains verifiable and reproducible, and suitable for comparative analysis across configuration changes.

**Alignment of Educational Assessment.**
The assessment system also draws on methodologies used in evaluating short-answer learning tasks, where validity judgments based on assessment criteria and traceable evidence play a pivotal role [3]. This alignment supports the practical measurement of educational utility rather than relying solely on the criteria of general language models.

Tool-Supported Assessment (Future Development).** By incorporating tool capabilities into MCP, concepts from realistic agent assessment—such as controlled tool invocation and measurable task completion—expand the assessment framework. In this context, assessment texts can be presented as MCP tools (e.g., `run_evaluation`) while maintaining the same structured input/output system to ensure transparency and auditability [10].

Overall, assessment tools are designed to function as **measurement tools**, not experimental tools, enabling systematic reflection on design trade-offs and supporting defensible conclusions in later project phases.

## 4.2 Infrastructure Dependencies

### 4.2.1 Deployment and Runtime Architecture

The objective of this section is to describe **how the system operates at runtime**, distinguishing explicitly between **query-time execution** and **ingestion-time processing**. The focus is operational rather than conceptual, reflecting how system components interact under real execution conditions [2].

#### (A) Compute Environment

**Development and pilot execution.**  
During development and early experimentation, system components are executed locally to enable rapid iteration, debugging, and controlled testing. A limited cloud-based deployment is used in parallel to allow supervisor review and to validate deployment assumptions under realistic network and resource constraints.

**Target deployment architecture.**  
In the target deployment, system components are deployed as **containerized cloud services** with clearly separated responsibilities:

- **`api-service`**:  
    A FastAPI-based service responsible for handling user queries, managing request validation, orchestrating retrieval and generation steps, and returning responses with citations.
    
- **`ingestion-worker`**:  
    A dedicated service responsible for document ingestion, OCR processing, and preprocessing. This component operates asynchronously and independently from user queries, enabling knowledge base updates without impacting query-time latency.
    
- **`vector-store`**:  
    A persistent vector database service (ChromaDB) that stores embeddings and metadata and serves similarity search requests during query-time retrieval.
    

This separation allows ingestion-time workloads (OCR, preprocessing, indexing) to be decoupled from query-time workloads (retrieval and generation), reducing contention and improving operational stability.

**Query-time execution path.**  
At query time, only the `api-service` and `vector-store` are involved. The system retrieves relevant evidence from the vector store, constructs a constrained prompt, invokes the language model, and returns a grounded answer. No OCR or re-indexing is performed during this phase, ensuring predictable latency characteristics.

**Ingestion-time execution path.**  
At ingestion time, the `ingestion-worker` processes PDF documents, applies OCR where required, generates structured outputs with metadata, and updates the vector store. This process may be triggered manually or in batches and is isolated from user-facing services to prevent ingestion-related delays from affecting interactive usage.

**GPU considerations.**  
No GPU requirement is assumed a priori. The necessity of GPU acceleration for OCR or embedding generation is deferred to GP2 and will be determined empirically based on measured processing time, cost, and throughput. Declaring CPU-only sufficiency or mandatory GPU usage without quantitative evaluation is explicitly avoided, as such assumptions introduce unnecessary evaluation risk.


#### (b) Storage

In GP1, the system does not adhere to a definitive storage architecture. Instead, it documents a clear storage plan to explicitly define the location and lifecycle of data, thus avoiding any ambiguity regarding the location of various data elements during operation and evaluation.

**Original PDF Sources.** The original curriculum PDFs are stored in a persistent storage layer (e.g., a cloud object container or a persistent storage unit). The storage technology and configuration are defined in GP2, based on deployment constraints, access patterns, and cost considerations.

**OCR Outputs and Metadata.** Text extracted from PDFs, along with its associated metadata (e.g., document ID, page number, and type), is stored separately from the original files. This data can be represented as structured files (e.g., JSON or Parquet) or within a document-oriented data store. Separating the OCR outputs from the raw PDFs enables data re-indexing and experimentation without the need to repeat costly extraction steps.

**Implications and Vector Index:** Vector inclusions and their associated metadata are stored in a **persistent copy of the ChromaDB database**, which serves as the system's primary knowledge index for retrieval. Continuous storage ensures the index can be reused during system restarts and evaluations.

**Process Logs:** Process logs are written to structured log files during the initial phase of development (GP1) to support debugging, performance analysis, and evaluation. In later phases, these logs may be consolidated into a centralized logging system to facilitate long-term monitoring and analysis.

Overall, this storage scheme establishes **clear data boundaries** between raw inputs, derived data, indexed representations, and process logs. By deferring specific technical commitments while documenting data flow and continuity requirements, the design maintains flexibility without leaving any lingering questions about data processing.

#### (c) External Services

The system relies on a limited set of external services to support core functionality during the first phase of development (GP1), with clear monitoring and management of reliability risks.

Language Model Provider. During the first phase of development (GP1), the system uses the OpenAI API as an external language model provider. API usage is treated as a monitored dependency, not a fixed assumption. Call rate, token consumption, latency, and error rates are logged through structured logs to enable cost control, performance analysis, and informed decisions regarding provider replacement or migration in later phases.

Cloud Hosting Platform. System components are deployed on a cloud hosting platform chosen based on availability, financial constraints, and ease of operation. The specific provider is not determined in the first phase of development (GP1), as the hosting choice does not affect the underlying architectural assumptions. This abstraction allows the system to be deployed on alternative platforms without redesigning the application logic.

**Authentication and Access Management (Later Stages)** User authentication and authorization are not mandatory in GP1 and are therefore deferred. As the system develops toward production deployment, standard mechanisms such as JWT-based or OAuth-based authentication can be introduced to support user accounts, access control, and usage policies. These mechanisms have been deliberately excluded from the scope of GP1 to avoid early complexity.

By limiting external service dependencies to those that are essential and observable, the implementation maintains **operational transparency** while preserving flexibility for future deployment and scaling decisions.

#### (d) Network and Security

**Network Dependence** The system requires a **constant internet connection** as a minimum requirement for accessing cloud language model services. Therefore, network availability is treated as an explicit operational dependency, not an implicit assumption. The current scope does not support offline operation.

**Transport Layer Security (TLS)** In the on-premise and early development stages, the use of Transport Layer Security (TLS) is not enforced. However, TLS is **mandatory for any official or externally available deployment**, particularly when transmitting user queries, retrieved content references, or API credentials. The absence of TLS in the prototype phase is documented as a scope constraint, not a design omission.

**Confidential Data Management** API keys and other sensitive credentials are not included in the source code or configuration files added to the version control system. Instead, confidential data is managed through environment variables or confidential data management mechanisms provided by the platform. Popular cloud deployment environments (such as Replit platforms) offer practical methods for isolating confidential data from application logic while maintaining ease of deployment [19]. This approach reduces the risk of credential leakage and supports safer iteration during development and pilot operation.

Overall, network and security considerations are treated as **operational controls** that scale with deployment maturity: minimal but explicit safeguards in GP1, and enforced transport security and hardened secret management in any release-level deployment.

### 4.2.2 Runtime Diagram

This section describes the **operational runtime flows** of the system, distinguishing explicitly between **query-time execution** and **ingestion-time processing**. The purpose is to clarify how data and control move across system components during normal operation.

#### Query-Time Execution Path

At query time, the system follows a streamlined, latency-sensitive execution path:

`Student` → `Web UI` → `Backend API` → `Retriever (ChromaDB)` → `LLM` → `Answer + Citations`

A student submits a curriculum-related question through the web interface, which forwards the request to the backend API. The backend normalizes the query and invokes the retriever to perform a top-_k_ semantic search over the vector database. Retrieved evidence chunks are then incorporated into a constrained prompt and passed to the language model. The model generates a response grounded in the retrieved evidence, and the backend returns the final answer together with explicit citations (document and page references) to the user interface.

This path excludes any OCR or indexing operations to ensure predictable response time and stable interactive performance.

#### Ingestion-Time Execution Path

Ingestion-time processing follows a separate, asynchronous workflow that is isolated from user interaction:

`PDFs (scanned)` → `Docling OCR` → `Clean / Normalize` → `Chunking` → `Embeddings` → `ChromaDB Index`

Scanned or heterogeneous PDF documents are first processed by the OCR engine to extract machine-readable text. The extracted content is then cleaned and normalized to reduce noise before being segmented into retrievable chunks. Embeddings are generated for each chunk and stored in the persistent vector database, updating the system’s knowledge index. This process can be executed in batches and does not interfere with query-time operations.

#### Operational Rationale

Separating query-time and ingestion-time paths ensures that computationally expensive tasks (such as OCR and embedding generation) do not affect interactive usage. Additionally, the explicit presentation of citations in the query-time output serves as a **trust-regulation mechanism** for both students and teachers, reducing the impact of non-verifiable or misleading answers by enabling direct inspection of the underlying sources [1], [13].

## 4.3 Trade-offs 

## 4.3 Trade-offs (Design Decisions and Compromises)

The implementation involves a set of **explicit trade-offs**, where certain options were selected while others were intentionally deferred or rejected. These decisions reflect technical, operational, and evaluation-driven considerations rather than assumptions of optimality. Table 4.2 summarizes the key trade-offs adopted in GP1 and their anticipated impact.

**Table 4.2 — Key Implementation Trade-offs**

| Decision area              | Option chosen                             | Option rejected           | Technical rationale                                                         | Expected impact                              |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| **LLM runtime (GP1)**      | OpenAI API                                | Local LLM from day one    | Reduces early implementation risk and stabilizes UX during prototyping      | Internet dependency and operational cost     |
| **Long-term LLM strategy** | Open-source LLM on cloud (target)         | Permanent vendor lock-in  | Improves long-term cost efficiency and reduces provider dependency          | Requires GPU provisioning and quality tuning |
| **OCR tooling**            | Docling-based pipeline                    | Manual extraction         | Data are predominantly scanned; manual workflows do not scale [2]           | OCR errors may propagate to retrieval        |
| **Embedding strategy**     | Multilingual or dual-model (GP2 decision) | Single untested model     | Arabic retrieval performance may degrade without language-aware tuning [13] | Direct impact on recall and precision        |
| **Vector store**           | ChromaDB (local, persistent)              | Managed vector DB (early) | Minimizes cost and complexity during pilot phase                            | Lower scalability ceiling in later stages    |
| **Retrieval depth**        | Empirically tuned top-_k_                 | Fixed arbitrary _k_       | _k_ controls the balance between recall and latency [1], [6]                | Large _k_ increases response time            |
| **Reranking**              | Deferred to GP2                           | Always-on reranking       | Adds latency and cost; enabled only after measured benefit [6]              | Potential latency degradation                |
| **Tool integration**       | MCP-based tools                           | Ad hoc SDK integration    | Standardization improves interoperability and maintainability [14]          | Larger attack surface if misconfigured [7]   |
| **Tool permissions**       | Allowlist + least privilege               | Broad permissions         | Reduces risk of tool misuse and injection attacks [7]                       | May constrain feature flexibility            |

These trade-offs highlight that the system is designed to be **incrementally optimized** rather than maximized prematurely. Each deferred or constrained choice preserves a clear path for future enhancement once empirical evidence from GP2 justifies increased complexity or resource investment.

## 4.4 Dependencies and Assumptions

### 4.4.1 Dependencies

#### (A) Runtime Software Dependencies

The proposed system relies on a set of runtime components that must be available and properly configured to handle both queries and input data. These dependencies are treated as essential operational requirements, not implementation details.

- **Python Runtime and Supporting Libraries:**

	The system requires a Python runtime environment and libraries for PDF parsing, optical character recognition (OCR) processing, embedding, and vector-based retrieval. Dependency versions are managed through a reproducible environment configuration, which is completed in GP2.

- **Back-End Framework and Configuration:**

	The back-end service is based on a FastAPI-based application layer, supported by external configuration files and environment variables (such as `.env`) that define runtime parameters, service endpoints, and resource limits.

- **Persistent Vector Database Service:**

	ChromaDB must be available and configured in **persistent mode** to store embeds and metadata after a reboot. This persistence layer is essential for maintaining a stable knowledge index during evaluation and piloting.

- **External Access Credentials to LLM:**

	Valid API credentials are required for the OpenAI service during GP1. The absence or misconfiguration of these credentials prevents the generation of responses during queries and is therefore a prerequisite for operation.

- **MCP Services (When Enabled):**

	When Model Context Protocol (MCP) integration is enabled, one or more MCP servers must be available and accessible within a trusted network environment. These servers provide clearly defined tools, which are essential for any tool-dependent implementation paths.

These conditions define the **minimum prerequisites** for system implementation. Any failure or misconfiguration at this level will result in performance degradation or complete service failure and is therefore closely monitored during deployment and testing.

#### (B) External Services and Infrastructure Dependencies

In addition to the operating software components, the system relies on a limited set of external services and infrastructure capabilities necessary for normal operation.

- **Internet Connection:**

A constant internet connection is required when using a cloud-based language model provider. Connection interruptions directly impact query execution and prevent response generation. This dependency is treated as an explicit operational requirement, not an implied assumption.

- **Container Execution Platform with Persistent Storage:**

Upon deployment, the system requires an execution platform that supports containers and persistent storage. Persistent storage is essential for preserving original documents, OCR outputs, vector indexes, and logs after a service restart. The platform and backend storage are selected at launch based on availability and budget constraints.

These external dependencies define the minimum core infrastructure required for deployment. While they do not affect the system's logical design, their availability directly determines the system's reliability and evaluability during trial operation.

#### (c) Key and Credential Management

All sensitive credentials required for system operation—such as third-party API keys—are managed exclusively through environment-level configuration mechanisms. Confidential credentials are added during operation via environment variables or confidential data managers provided by the platform and are never included in the source code or added to the version control repository.

This approach minimizes the risk of credential leaks during development, testing, or deployment and aligns with common security practices for cloud and containerized applications. Credential management is treated as an operational requirement, not an application feature, and must be properly configured to ensure successful system operation.

In the initial beta (GP1) release, this mechanism is sufficient for prototyping and testing. More advanced credential rotation mechanisms or identity-based access controls can only be added as the system evolves to be production-ready.

The system's operation generally relies on an optical character recognition (OCR) document processing workflow, which extracts curriculum content from scanned PDF files and converts it into retrievable text segments using vector embeddings stored in a persistent vector database. During query execution, the system relies on the availability of this indexed content to retrieve reference segments and generate evidence-supported answers via a linguistic model. Access to required external services is treated as an operational dependency and is managed through environment variables rather than embedded credentials, ensuring that sensitive access keys remain outside the codebase and are configurable upon deployment.

### 4.4.2 Assumptions

#### (A) Presumptions Regarding Input PDFs  
  
The implementation presupposes that the curriculum PDF files utilized as input meet a series of **minimum operational criteria** necessary for dependable ingestion and processing:  
The PDF files are machine-readable and intact, facilitating automated parsing and processing at the page level.  
- The scanned documents demonstrate adequate visual quality for OCR extraction. The specific resolution thresholds and preprocessing settings will be determined by GP2 and finalized by empirical assessment.  
- Document content is available in either Arabic or English, in accordance with the system's supported language parameters.  
- Legal permission is granted to process, index, and reference the curriculum and educational resources utilized as input sources.  
  
  
These assumptions delineate the **operational envelope** within which the system is anticipated to operate effectively. Noncompliance with these conditions may lead to diminished ingestion quality or system malfunction and is hence regarded as a precondition rather than an error case in GP1.  
  
  
#### (B) Presumptions Regarding System Environment  
  
The system functioning presupposes the presence of **ample computational and storage resources** to sustain the vector index and related information, including sufficient RAM and enduring storage capacity. Resource allocation is anticipated to correspond with the magnitude of the indexed curriculum content during the pilot phase.  
  
Furthermore, the approach presupposes that **pilot usage volume maintains below acceptable limits**, ensuring that simultaneous queries and ingestion processes do not beyond the capability of the implemented infrastructure. This assumption is essential to prevent unregulated deterioration in response latency or operational expenses during GP1, where assertive autoscaling and cost-optimization strategies are not yet implemented.  
  
These assumptions delineate the environmental parameters within which system performance and cost dynamics can be effectively assessed before scaling in subsequent project phases.  
  
  
  
#### (C) Presumptions Regarding User Conduct  
  
The system presumes that user interactions, especially student inquiries, stay **within the parameters of the indexed curriculum content**. This assumption is essential for ensuring answer dependability and reducing the possibility of hallucination. When inquiries exceed the indexed parameters, the system is programmed to explicitly recognize its constraints by either declining or redirecting, rather than engaging in conjectural production.  
  
User behavior directly impacts system efficacy, assessment validity, and perceived reliability from an operational standpoint. Table 4.3 encapsulates the principal behavioral and environmental assumptions, their practical significance, and the repercussions of their infringement.

**Table 4.3 — User and Environment Assumptions and Their Implications**

|Assumption|Why it matters|What happens if violated|
|---|---|---|
|PDFs are OCR-readable|OCR quality underpins retrieval accuracy|OCR errors → weak retrieval → misleading answers|
|Queries are within indexed curriculum scope|Limits hallucination and enables grounding|Unsupported answers or explicit refusal|
|Internet connectivity is available|Required for LLM API access|Query-time failure|
|Secrets are correctly managed|Prevents credential leakage and abuse|Security breach or unexpected billing|

These assumptions clarify that system correctness is not solely a function of model performance, but also of **usage discipline and operational context**. Making such assumptions explicit is essential for fair evaluation and for interpreting failure modes observed during pilot deployment.

1. **Clear delineation of the technological stack:**  
	Every layer of the system stack is linked to a distinctly specified tool or library, a well-founded justification for its choice, and a recorded strategy for version locking in GP2.  
	  
2. **Description of Operational Runtime:**  
	A runtime diagram or similar description is presented, clearly differentiating between the ingestion-time pipeline and the query-time execution pathway.

	The characteristics of the input data, primarily scanned PDF documents, are explicitly delineated, together with a technical rationale for the essential role of OCR in ensuring retrieval and answer quality.

	System dependencies and operational assumptions are articulated as clear constraints that govern proper system functionality, rather than as implicit assertions or assurances.
